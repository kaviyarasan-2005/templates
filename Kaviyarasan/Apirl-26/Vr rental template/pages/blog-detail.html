The Ultimate Yellowstone RV Guide: 14 Days, 500 Miles & Every Geyser Basin

From the steaming terraces of Mammoth to the prismatic fire of Grand Prismatic Spring — Yellowstone is America's most spectacular RV destination. But navigating it well requires planning. Here's everything we've learned from a decade of trips.

When to Visit Yellowstone in an RV

The single most important decision you'll make is timing. Yellowstone receives 4+ million visitors annually, and the experience varies wildly by season.

  • June: Waterfalls at peak flow from snowmelt, wildflowers blooming, moderate crowds. Our top pick for first-timers.
  • July–August: Peak wildlife viewing, all roads open, but campgrounds book out 6 months in advance.
  • September: Elk rut begins, crowds thin, temperatures drop sharply at night. Superb photography light.
  • October: Heavy snowfall possible, some facilities close, but solitude and drama abound.
Pro Tip

Book your campsite the second reservations open — which is exactly 6 months before your travel date at 8:00 AM Mountain Time on Recreation.gov. Set multiple phone alarms.

Best Campgrounds for RVs

Not all Yellowstone campgrounds can handle large rigs. Here's our tested breakdown:

  • Madison Campground: The undisputed champion. Central location, pull-through sites up to 40ft, fire rings, flush toilets. Book 6 months out.
  • Fishing Bridge RV Park: The only site with full hook-ups (water/electric/sewer). Premium pricing but worth it for long stays.
  • Grant Village: Near Lewis Lake and Yellowstone Lake. Quieter than Madison, good for 35ft rigs.
  • Canyon Village: Closest to Grand Canyon of Yellowstone. Limited large-rig sites — arrive early.

The 14-Day Itinerary

Days 1–3: Gateway & Arrival

Fly into Bozeman, MT or Jackson Hole, WY. Pick up your rig from our Denver or Salt Lake City locations for a scenic drive-in. Use these days to stock provisions, calibrate your systems, and get comfortable with driving.

Days 4–6: Geyser Country

The Old Faithful area is the heart of Yellowstone and deserves at least three days. Beyond the iconic geyser itself, hike to Observation Point (1.8 miles round-trip) for an aerial perspective that most visitors miss entirely. The Grand Prismatic Spring boardwalk is a must-do early morning when steam drifts dramatically across the rainbow colors.

Days 7–9: Grand Canyon of Yellowstone

The 24-mile-long canyons, carved by the Yellowstone River, reveal walls of brilliant yellow rhyolite. Hike down to the brink of the Lower Falls for one of the most dramatic vistas in all of America. Artists Point at sunrise deserves a full hour — bring a tripod.

Days 10–12: Lamar Valley & Wildlife

Known as "America's Serengeti" — the wide grassland valley where bison herds number in the thousands and wolf packs actively hunt. Arrive before dawn and stay until dark. The Slough Creek Trail offers excellent wolf-watching overlooks.

Wildlife Safety

Maintain 100-yard distance from wolves and bears, 25 yards from all other wildlife. Never approach bison — they're responsible for more Yellowstone injuries than any predator.

Days 13–14: Mammoth & Departure

End your journey at Mammoth Hot Springs, whose terraced travertine formations are unlike anything else in the park. The town of Gardiner, MT just 5 miles north has excellent restaurants and is your easiest exit north.

RV-Specific Driving Tips

  • The speed limit throughout Yellowstone is 45 mph — enforce it. Wildlife on roads is constant.
  • Most tunnels in the region have clearance restrictions. Know your rig height (our RVs all come with height stickers on the dash).
  • Fill your propane before entering the park — there are no propane stations inside.
  • Generator use is restricted at many campgrounds. Solar power or the Fishing Bridge hookups are your best bet.
  • Download the Yellowstone app and NPS maps offline before you lose cell service at the entrance.
