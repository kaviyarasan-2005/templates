The Art of the Perfect Pour-Over

Master the pour-over technique with our step-by-step guide. From water temperature to pour patterns, every detail matters for extracting the best flavors.

The pour-over is one of the most rewarding brewing methods in specialty coffee. It gives you complete control over every variable — grind size, water temperature, pour rate, and timing — allowing you to extract the full flavor potential of any bean.

What You'll Need

  • Pour-over dripper (Hario V60, Kalita Wave, or Chemex)
  • Paper or metal filter
  • Gooseneck kettle with temperature control
  • Burr grinder (hand or electric)
  • Digital scale (0.1g precision)
  • Fresh coffee beans (roasted within 2 weeks)
  • Timer

The Golden Ratio

For most pour-over methods, we recommend starting with a 1:16 coffee-to-water ratio. That's 15g of coffee to 240g (ml) of water. Adjust from there based on your taste preference — use more coffee for a stronger, richer cup, or more water for something lighter.

"The perfect pour-over isn't about following rules perfectly — it's about understanding the variables so you can adjust them to your personal taste." — Sophie Chen, Head Barista at Ember & Brew

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Heat your water to 200-205°F (93-96°C). If you don't have a thermometer, bring water to a boil and let it rest for 30 seconds.
  2. Grind your coffee to a medium-fine consistency, similar to table salt. For a V60, aim for a slightly finer grind; for a Chemex, go slightly coarser.
  3. Rinse the filter with hot water. This removes any paper taste and preheats your dripper and mug. Discard the rinse water.
  4. Add coffee to the filter and give it a gentle shake to level the bed. Place everything on your scale and tare to zero.
  5. The bloom: Start your timer and pour 30-45g of water in concentric circles, wetting all the grounds evenly. Wait 30-45 seconds as the coffee "blooms" and releases CO2.
  6. The pour: Begin your main pour in slow, steady concentric circles from the center outward. Avoid pouring directly on the filter paper. Pour in 3-4 stages, allowing the water to draw down slightly between pours.
  7. Target total brew time: 2:30-3:30 for a V60, 3:30-4:30 for a Chemex. If your brew is too fast, grind finer. Too slow? Go coarser.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Using Stale Coffee

Coffee is best enjoyed 7-21 days after roasting. Beyond that, the volatile aromatics that make pour-over so vibrant begin to fade. That's why all Ember & Brew subscription coffees arrive within days of roasting.

2. Inconsistent Grind Size

A blade grinder produces uneven particles, leading to both over- and under-extraction in the same cup. Invest in a burr grinder — even a hand-crank model like the Hario Skerton makes a dramatic difference.

3. Pouring Too Fast

Aggressive, fast pouring agitates the coffee bed too much and leads to channeling. Keep your pour gentle and controlled. A gooseneck kettle is essential for this precision.
